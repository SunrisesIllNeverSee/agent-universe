## PRICING PHILOSOPHY

Two products. One wave cascade system. Different value arguments.

**COMMAND** sells operational infrastructure — a better way to run AI at governance depth.  
**DEPLOY** sells outcomes — campaigns, formations, missions, wins.

Outcomes command a higher price floor. A financial operation returning $500K through a DEPLOY campaign makes the license fee a rounding error. Pricing reflects this. DEPLOY market rates sit above COMMAND at every wave — intentionally.

## DISTRIBUTION

Not a seat. Not a wave product. Invitation only — never advertised publicly.

A standing distribution agreement. Partner carries COMMAND and DEPLOY into markets Ello Cello LLC is not actively covering. MO§ES™ brand stays on the product. Royalty flows back on every license or campaign generated through their channel.

**Not white-label.** Brand does not change. Not capped — multiple partners possible. Scoped to avoid white-label territory overlap.

**Transferability:**  
Distribution agreements are transferable — but on entirely separate terms from the wave cascade. There is no matched pricing, no wave-rate reference, and no seat registry equivalence. A distribution agreement transfer reflects the value of the distribution rights themselves — the channel, the territory, the revenue stream — not the underlying product license. Transfer terms are bespoke, negotiated case by case, and will typically exceed licensing seat transfer terms given the nature of what is being transferred. Ello Cello LLC receives royalty on all distribution agreement transfers.

## SEAT APPRECIATION MECHANIC
*Internal use only — not for public copy*

Every time a new wave opens at a higher price, every seat from every prior wave appreciates immediately. This is structural, not speculative. It is built into the cascade.

**The mechanism:**

When CW1a closes at $65,000 and CW2a opens at $195,000 — a CW1a holder now owns a seat whose entry replacement cost is $195,000. Their last confirmed sale price still reads $65,000 on the card. But anyone who wants in *right now* pays $195,000 minimum through the cascade. The CW1a seat didn't change. The market floor around it did.

The secondary market price for a CW1a seat at that point sits anywhere between $65,000 and $195,000 — and it's a better deal than what the cascade currently offers regardless of where in that range it lands. The wave structure creates the secondary market floor automatically.

**It compounds across every wave:**

| Moment | CW1a Seat Replacement Cost | Delta from Basis |
|---|---|---|
| CW1a open | $65,000 | — |
| CW1b opens at $130,000 | $130,000 | +$65,000 |
| CW2a opens at $195,000 | $195,000 | +$130,000 |
| CW2b opens at $260,000 | $260,000 | +$195,000 |
| CW3 opens — BID | BID floor | +BID floor |

By the time CW2b opens, a CW1a holder sitting on a $65,000 basis owns a seat in a registry where new entry costs $260,000. The appreciation is real, confirmed, and visible — not projected.

**The transfer notation makes it legible:**

CI¹ with a confirmed transfer price of $140,000 tells the next buyer exactly what happened — someone bought at $65,000, the market moved through two wave openings, and they captured the delta at transfer. The registry records it. The history is permanent.

**Same logic carries across DEPLOY:**

DW1a seats at $95,000 market entry appreciate identically as DW2a opens at $275,000, DW2b at $370,000, and so on. DEPLOY appreciation runs on the same structural mechanic — higher price floor at each new wave opening immediately reprices all prior wave seats upward.

**For bundle holders:**

A CW1a + DW1a bundle at $130,000 combined ($65K + $65K) sits against a replacement cost of $195,000 + $275,000 = $470,000 the moment CW2a and DW2a open. That's $340,000 of structural appreciation on a $130,000 entry. The bundle compounds the mechanic across both products simultaneously.

**Key principle:**

The cascade doesn't just create urgency at the point of sale. It creates ongoing, compounding value for early holders at every subsequent wave opening — automatically, without any action required on their part. Early entry is not just the lowest price. It is the highest appreciation position.

This is the unspoken argument for Wave 1. It doesn't need to be said publicly. It needs to be understood internally and surfaced selectively in buyer conversations where it's relevant.
